# 1. Introduction

Stroke is the second leading cause of death globally, responsible for approximately 6.5 million deaths annually and leaving an additional 5 million people with permanent disability [WHO 2022, WSO 2022]. Each year, an estimated 13 million people experience a new stroke event, imposing a substantial burden on healthcare systems worldwide. Early identification of at-risk individuals is essential for preventive intervention, and machine learning (ML) has been widely proposed as a tool for automated stroke risk prediction from clinical data [Chadha 2024, Asadi 2024].

The volume of ML-based stroke prediction research has grown rapidly, with over 50 studies published since 2017 across journals spanning computer science, clinical informatics, and neurology [Chadha 2024]. These studies consistently report high predictive accuracy — frequently exceeding 95% — using models such as Random Forest, XGBoost, and logistic regression. Taken at face value, these results suggest that the problem of stroke prediction is largely solved. However, a closer examination of this literature reveals a troubling pattern of methodological convergence that obscures the true difficulty of the task.

We systematically reviewed 13 recent papers — 11 modeling studies and 2 systematic reviews — and found that nine of eleven modeling papers rely on the same Kaggle Stroke Prediction Dataset, a publicly available file of 5,110 rows with 10 self-reported features and no laboratory measurements. The dataset's provenance is undocumented, and its features exclude clinically validated stroke risk factors such as measured blood pressure, cholesterol, atrial fibrillation, and medication history. The dominant evaluation metric across these studies is accuracy, which is fundamentally misleading on imbalanced data: at the 4–5% stroke prevalence typical of these datasets, a model that predicts "no stroke" for every patient achieves over 95% accuracy. Three papers in our review independently demonstrated this phenomenon, reporting models with 95–96% accuracy that detected zero stroke cases in the test set [Wu and Fang 2020, Frontiers 2025, Scientific Reports 2025]. Despite these findings, subsequent papers continued to report accuracy as the headline metric without acknowledging its limitations [Sinkron 2025, Diagnostics 2024, Sensors 2025].

Beyond the accuracy trap, no modeling paper in our review validated on an independent dataset, compared against established clinical risk scores such as the Framingham Stroke Risk Profile [Wolf 1991], evaluated performance across demographic subgroups, or reported probability calibration. The result is a body of literature that benchmarks similar models on the same small dataset, reports optimistic metrics, and provides no evidence of real-world clinical utility. This is not a criticism of any individual study, but a structural observation about a field that has optimized for publication metrics rather than clinical relevance.

In this study, we apply the same models that dominate the literature — logistic regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost — to a fundamentally different dataset: six cycles of the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES, 2007–2018), comprising 34,719 adults with 1,398 stroke cases and 22 clinically measured features including laboratory values, examiner-measured blood pressure, comorbidity history, and sociodemographic variables. We evaluate these models with temporal validation (training on 2007–2014, testing on 2015–2018), PR-AUC as the primary metric, bootstrap 95% confidence intervals, DeLong tests for pairwise model comparison, a simplified Framingham-like risk score baseline, and fairness analysis by sex and race/ethnicity. We follow the TRIPOD reporting guidelines [Collins 2015] and release all code and data publicly.

Our results are sobering. The best model achieves a PR-AUC of 0.162 (95% CI: 0.142–0.188) — an honest measure of performance on a problem with 4% prevalence. This barely exceeds a simple clinical risk score (PR-AUC = 0.144). No model is statistically distinguishable from any other on ROC-AUC (DeLong p > 0.74 for all pairs). Recall ranges from 49% for Hispanic participants to 76% for Non-Hispanic White participants, revealing a fairness gap that no prior study measured. These findings do not mean ML is useless for stroke prediction — they mean the field's current approach is inadequate, and honest evaluation is the first step toward genuine progress.
