# 6. Conclusion

We evaluated three widely used machine learning models for stroke prediction on NHANES clinical data (N = 34,719; 1,398 stroke cases) with temporal validation, clinically meaningful metrics, a Framingham baseline comparison, and fairness analysis — elements absent from the existing literature. Our findings reveal that the field's reported 95–99% accuracy rates are artifacts of class imbalance and evaluation methodology, not genuine predictive power. The best model achieves a PR-AUC of 0.162 (95% CI: 0.142–0.188), marginally above a simple clinical risk score (0.144), with no statistically significant difference between models and a 27-percentage-point recall gap across racial subgroups. These results demonstrate that advancing stroke prediction requires not better algorithms but better data, better evaluation, and honest reporting. All code and data are publicly available to support reproducibility and future work.
