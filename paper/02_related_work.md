# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Machine Learning for Stroke Prediction

Machine learning approaches to stroke prediction have expanded rapidly since 2017, with Random Forest emerging as the most frequently used and best-performing algorithm across studies [Chadha 2024, Asadi 2024]. The majority of modeling studies rely on the Kaggle Stroke Prediction Dataset, a publicly available tabular dataset of 5,110 records with 10 features including age, sex, hypertension, heart disease, marital status, work type, residence type, average glucose level, BMI, and smoking status. Among the 11 modeling papers in our review, nine used this dataset or a near-identical variant [Sinkron 2025, IJACSA 2021, Gao 2024, Diagnostics 2024, Frontiers 2025, Frontiers 2026, Sensors 2022, Sensors 2025, Scientific Reports 2025]. Only Wu and Fang [2020] used a clinical cohort — the Chinese Longitudinal Healthy Longevity Study (N = 1,131) — and only one study used hospital medical records from a single institution in Tehran (N = 663) [Scientific Reports 2024].

Reported accuracy across these studies ranges from 82% [IJACSA 2021] to 99.2% [Sensors 2025], and ROC-AUC from 0.82 to 1.00. Several papers proposed increasingly complex architectures to push these numbers higher: Dritsas and Trigka [2022] used stacking ensembles with four base classifiers and a logistic regression meta-learner, achieving 98% accuracy. Sensors [2025] combined Random Forest and LightGBM through a deep neural network meta-classifier with SMOTE-SMOTEENN hybrid resampling, reporting 99.2% accuracy and AUC of 1.000. Gao and Wang [2024] employed WGAN-GP for synthetic data augmentation paired with a 7-layer deep regression network, reporting an F-score of 0.97. These results suggest near-perfect prediction, but as we discuss below, methodological issues undermine their credibility.

## 2.2 Methodological Concerns in the Literature

Three papers in our review independently demonstrated that high accuracy on imbalanced stroke data is illusory. Wu and Fang [2020] found that all three of their models achieved 90–95% accuracy on the CLHLS dataset but detected no stroke cases, with sensitivity approximately 0.00 and AUC approximately 0.50. The Frontiers in Neurology study [2025] reported that logistic regression achieved 95.11% accuracy with an ROC-AUC of 0.837 on the Kaggle dataset, yet detected zero true positives out of 50 stroke cases. The Scientific Reports study [2025] confirmed the pattern with 96% accuracy and precision, recall, and F1 of zero for the stroke class. Despite these findings, subsequent papers continued to report accuracy as the headline metric without addressing its limitations in imbalanced settings.

Several papers applied SMOTE or other oversampling techniques with ambiguous methodology. Dritsas and Trigka [2022] and Sensors [2025] did not clearly state whether resampling was applied before or after cross-validation splits. If synthetic minority samples generated from the full dataset appear in both training and validation folds, performance estimates are inflated through data leakage. The near-perfect results reported by these papers are consistent with this leakage pattern. One study [Scientific Reports 2024] included aphasia, hemiparesis, and cerebrovascular accident as input features — clinical manifestations of stroke itself — constituting target leakage that directly explains the reported 99.9% accuracy.

External validation is absent across the field. Not one of the eleven modeling papers validated on an independent dataset; every reported metric comes from the same data source used for training. Both systematic reviews confirmed this as a field-wide pattern: Chadha [2024] found that external validation was absent across all 31 reviewed studies, and Asadi et al. [2024] reported the same across 20 studies.

## 2.3 Systematic Reviews

Two systematic reviews provide field-level assessments. Chadha [2024] reviewed 31 studies (2017–2023) using PRISMA guidelines and found that Random Forest was the most popular and best-performing model, that the Kaggle dataset dominated the field, and that external validation and clinical deployment were universally absent. Asadi et al. [2024] reviewed 20 studies (2019–2023) and reported that Random Forest was the best algorithm in 25% of articles, that 10 of 20 studies used Kaggle data, and that age was the most consistently significant predictive feature. Neither review assessed calibration, fairness, or comparison against established clinical risk scores.

## 2.4 Identified Gaps

Our review identified six critical gaps that persist across the literature. No study validated on an independent or temporally separated dataset. Nine of eleven studies relied on the same Kaggle dataset with its limited 10-feature schema. Accuracy remained the dominant metric despite repeated demonstrations of its inadequacy for imbalanced data. No study compared ML performance against clinical risk scores such as the Framingham Stroke Risk Profile or CHA₂DS₂-VASc. No study evaluated whether models performed equitably across demographic subgroups. And only one study [Frontiers 2026] reported any calibration metric, with none reporting calibration curves. The present study was designed to address these gaps systematically using a single, publicly available clinical dataset and transparent methodology.
